Lead‑Acid (Car) Battery

Yoosuf Mohamed

1. Required Information

a. Common Names
  • Lead–acid battery
  • Car / SLI battery (Starting‑Lighting‑Ignition)
  • Storage battery
  • Flooded lead–acid (FLA) & Valve‑Regulated Lead–Acid (VRLA) which includes AGM and Gel types
b. Main Applications
  • Automotive SLI: Delivers a quick surge (≈ 300 A) to crank internal‑combustion engines and powers headlights/radios when the alternator is off.
  • Industrial Traction: Multi‑cell packs drive forklifts, golf carts, scissor lifts where low‑cost deep‑cycle capacity is needed.
  • Stationary Backup / UPS: VRLA batteries stabilize computer‑server loads or telecom towers during grid outages.
  • Renewable‑Energy Storage: Off‑grid solar & small wind systems employ deep‑cycle lead–acid banks as a budget alternative to Li‑ion.
c. Pictures & Uses (4)
d. Materials (Cell Components) Anode (negative during discharge): spongy Pb(s) plates cast onto a Pb–Sb or Pb–Ca grid.
Cathode (positive): pressed PbO2(s) active mass supported by a Pb grid.
Electrolyte: 30–40 wt % aqueous H2SO4; density drops from ≈1.28 g mL−1 (charged) to ≈1.12 g mL−1 (discharged).
Separator: microporous PVC, polyethylene, or AGM fiberglass that allows ion conduction but prevents plate contact.
Container & Venting: polypropylene case with flame‑arrestor caps; VRLA designs use pressure‑relief valves to recombine evolved gases.
e. Standard Half‑Reactions (25 °C) Cathode (reduction):
PbO2(s) + HSO4− + 3 H+ + 2 e− → PbSO4(s) + 2 H2O(l) E° = +1.685 V

Anode (oxidation, i.e. reverse of reduction listed):
Pb(s) + HSO4− → PbSO4(s) + H+ + 2 e− E° (reduction) = −0.356 V
f. Overall Discharge Reaction & E°cell Pb(s) + PbO2(s) + 2 H2SO4(aq) → 2 PbSO4(s) + 2 H2O(l)
E°cell = 1.685 − (−0.356) ≈ 2.04 V per cell (ΔG° = −nF E° ≈ −394 kJ mol−1). Six cells in series give the familiar 12 V nominal car battery.

2. Why It Works

  1. Galvanic Cell Principle: The spontaneous redox pair (Pb/PbSO4 & PbO2/PbSO4) has a positive E°cell, so ΔG° = −nF E° is negative, driving electrons from anode → cathode through the external circuit.
  2. Acid‑Dependent Electrode Chemistry: H+ ions appear in both half‑reactions; as discharge proceeds, [H+] decreases while water forms, lowering acid density—this provides a built‑in state‑of‑charge indicator.
  3. Sulfation & Reversibility: Lead‑sulfate forms on both electrodes. If left discharged for long, PbSO4 crystals grow large and resist reconversion, raising internal resistance—explaining why timely recharging is essential.
  4. Charge Reaction: Reapplying ≥ 2.2 V per cell forces PbSO4 back to Pb and PbO2, restoring acid concentration (Le Châtelier’s principle in action).
  5. Design Variations: Flooded cells allow water replacement; AGM/Gel immobilize electrolyte, permitting any orientation and reduced gas loss—ideal for UPS or marine settings.
